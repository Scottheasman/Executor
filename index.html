Pre-Probate — Do This First
Secure the home & gather documents
🔴 Urgent ›

What This Step Is

Before anything can happen legally, you need to stabilize the physical estate and gather the documents you'll need at the courthouse. Nothing else can move forward without the original Will and death certificates in hand.

Action Items

  • Go to the house and secure all entry points — change locks if needed
  • Set the thermostat to 55°F to protect pipes (important in Rochester winters)
  • Set up mail forwarding to your address (USPS.com or your local post office)
  • Find and safeguard the original Will — courts require the physical original, not a copy
  • Ask the funeral home for 10 certified death certificates — banks and courts each need an original
  • Collect every bill, statement, or official letter you find — put them in one box
Why 10 copies? Each bank, brokerage, insurance company, and the court requires their own certified original. You'll go through them faster than you think.

What This Step Is

This is when the estate officially enters the legal system. You file the original Will along with a "Petition for Probate" form at Monroe County Surrogate's Court. The court then formally recognizes you as the Executor.

Where To Go

Monroe County Surrogate's Court
99 Exchange Blvd, Room 514
Rochester, NY 14614
Hours: Mon–Fri, 9am–5pm

📍 Open in Maps

What You Need To Bring

  • The original Will (not a copy)
  • At least 2–3 certified death certificates
  • A completed Petition for Probate form (available at the clerk's window or online at nycourts.gov)
  • Filing fee — typically $45–$1,250 depending on estate size (paid from estate funds)
  • Names and addresses of all beneficiaries named in the Will
Tip: Call ahead at (585) 371-3609 to confirm current hours and ask if there's a checklist for first-time filers. The clerks are generally helpful.
2 Court
Receive Letters Testamentary
Your official legal authority as Executor
Weeks 2–4 ›

What This Step Is

"Letters Testamentary" is the official document issued by the court that proves you have legal authority to act on behalf of the estate. Without this document, banks and financial institutions will not speak with you about the accounts.

What Happens

  • After filing, the court typically takes 2–4 weeks to process and issue Letters
  • You'll receive a certified document with an official court seal
  • Order multiple certified copies — each institution (bank, brokerage, DMV) will want one
  • The court may require notifying beneficiaries by mail before issuing
This is the "golden ticket." Once you have it, you can legally access, manage, and eventually distribute all estate assets. Keep originals safe — make photocopies for reference.
3 Inventory
Inventory All Assets
List accounts, property, and notify institutions
Ongoing ›

What This Step Is

You need to create a complete picture of everything she owned — every bank account, investment, property, and insurance policy. The last 2 years of tax returns are the single best "map" of all her accounts.

What To Find

  • Bank accounts: Checking, savings, CDs — note if any are "Joint" or "POD" (payable on death), as those transfer automatically outside probate
  • Investments: 401(k), IRA, brokerage accounts, or physical stock certificates
  • Life insurance: Call each company with the policy number to file a claim
  • Real estate: Mortgage statements, property tax bills, any HELOC loans
  • Vehicle: Title, registration — contact NYS DMV to transfer
  • Debts: Credit cards, car loans, recent medical bills

Notify These Locally

  • Utilities — keep ON: RG&E (585-546-1900), Monroe County Water, trash pickup
  • Social Security Administration — notify of death to stop payments (return any received after death)
  • Post office — continue mail forwarding
4 Bank
Open an Estate Bank Account
Keep estate money separate from yours
After Letters ›

What This Step Is

You must open a bank account specifically in the name of the estate (e.g., "Estate of [Name]"). All estate money flows through this account — this keeps things clean, protects you legally, and makes accounting easy when it's time to close.

How To Do It

  • Bring your Letters Testamentary and your own ID to any bank
  • Request a basic estate checking account — no need for anything fancy
  • Get an EIN (Employer ID Number) for the estate at IRS.gov — takes 5 minutes online, free
  • Transfer her existing bank accounts into this estate account
Important: Never mix estate money with your personal money. Even reimbursing yourself for expenses should be documented and paid from the estate account with a receipt.
5 Debts
Settle Debts & Taxes
Pay valid claims from estate funds — not yours
Months 1–6 ›

What This Step Is

Before distributing anything to heirs, valid debts must be paid from the estate account. You are not personally responsible for her debts — creditors can only claim against estate assets, not yours.

What To Pay

  • Funeral and burial costs — these are paid first, by law
  • Final utility bills and mortgage — keep property expenses current
  • Valid credit card and medical debts — verify each one is legitimate
  • Federal and NY State final income taxes — file her last tax return (Form 1040) for the year she died
  • NY Estate Tax — only applies if estate exceeds ~$7.16 million (2024 threshold)

New York Notice to Creditors

NY law allows you to publish a "Notice to Creditors" in a local newspaper (like the Democrat & Chronicle). This starts a 7-month clock — creditors who don't file within that window lose their claim, protecting you when distributing.

If a debt seems wrong — inflated medical bills, accounts you don't recognize — you have the right to dispute them. An estate attorney can help if needed.
6 Final
Distribute Assets & Close Estate
Follow the Will, then file final court papers
✓ Finish Line ›

What This Step Is

Once all debts are paid and the creditor notice period has passed, you distribute what remains to the beneficiaries exactly as the Will directs. Then you file a final accounting with the court and formally close the estate.

Distribution Process

  • Pay each heir their share — get a signed receipt and release from each one
  • For real estate: you'll need to file a new deed at Monroe County Clerk's Office (39 W. Main St)
  • For vehicles: transfer title at the Monroe County DMV
  • Transfer remaining investment accounts per Will instructions

Closing the Estate

  • File a Final Account with Surrogate's Court showing all income, expenses, and distributions
  • Get the court's approval — they'll issue a discharge releasing you from Executor duties
  • Close the estate bank account
You're done. The discharge order is your official release from all Executor responsibilities. Keep all your records for at least 3–5 years.

If You Hire an Attorney

Estate Attorney Fees — Monroe County

Hourly billing: Most Rochester estate attorneys charge $200–$500/hour. For a simple, uncontested estate this often runs $1,500–$3,000 total.

Flat fee: Some offer a flat fee of $3,500+ for straightforward probate cases — often the better deal for peace of mind.

Who pays? Attorney fees are paid directly from the estate account — not from your personal money.

Ask: "Do you offer a flat fee for a simple probate in Monroe County?"
