Pre-Probate — Do This First
Secure the home & gather documents
🔴 Urgent ›

What This Step Is

Before anything can happen legally, you need to stabilize the physical estate and gather the documents you'll need at the courthouse. Nothing else can move forward without the original Will and death certificates in hand.

Action Items

  • Go to the house and secure all entry points — change locks if needed
  • Set thermostat to 55°F to protect pipes (critical in Rochester winters)
  • Set up mail forwarding to your address at USPS.com
  • Find and safeguard the original Will — courts require the physical original, not a copy
  • Ask the funeral home for 10 certified death certificates
  • Collect every bill, statement, or official letter — one box

Find the Will's Witnesses

Check this first: Look at the very end of the Will for a notarized self-proving affidavit signed by the witnesses at the time the Will was executed. If one is attached, the court can admit the Will without any witness tracking — skip the steps below entirely. Ask the attorney holding the Will: "Was a self-proving affidavit attached at signing?"

If there is no self-proving affidavit, the court requires a sworn Affidavit of Attesting Witnesses (Form P-3) from each person who signed the Will as a witness. Start tracking them down now — this takes time.

  • Check the Will itself — witness names and sometimes addresses are listed at the end
  • If a witness cannot be found, you'll file Form P-8 to ask the court to dispense with their testimony
  • If a witness is deceased, file both Form P-8 and Form P-9 (Affidavit Proving Handwriting)
  • Note: a witness cannot notarize another witness's affidavit — use a separate notary
Why 10 death certificates? Each bank, brokerage, insurance company, and the court requires their own certified original. You'll go through them faster than you think.

1Week 1
File at Surrogate's Court
Submit Will, Petition & all required forms
Next Week ›

What This Step Is

This is when the estate officially enters the legal system. You file the original Will along with a Petition for Probate at Monroe County Surrogate's Court. The court formally recognizes you as the Executor.

Where To Go

Monroe County Surrogate's Court
99 Exchange Blvd, Room 514
Rochester, NY 14614
Mon–Fri, 9am–5pm · (585) 371-3609

📍 Open in Maps

What To Bring

  • The original Will (not a copy — they keep it)
  • 2–3 certified death certificates minimum
  • Completed Petition for Probate (Form P-1) — from clerk's window or nycourts.gov
  • Affidavit of Attesting Witnesses (Form P-3) from each Will witness
  • Affidavit of Comparison (Form P-13) — swears a copy matches the original Will
  • Names, addresses, and relationships of all beneficiaries and distributees
  • Filing fee (see exact schedule below)

Exact Filing Fee Schedule (SCPA §2402)

Based on the gross value of assets the decedent owned individually:

Under $10,000$45
$10,000 – $19,999$75
$20,000 – $49,999$215
$50,000 – $99,999$280
$100,000 – $249,999$420
$250,000 – $499,999$625
$500,000 and over$1,250

+ $6 per certified copy of Letters Testamentary. Order at least 8–10 copies.

Bond — A Critical Decision

Unless every interested party signs a Waiver of Bond (Form P-11 / CTA-3), the court will require you to purchase a surety bond — an insurance policy protecting the estate. Bonds cost money (a % of estate value) and take time to obtain.

  • Get Consent to Dispense with Bond signed by all adult beneficiaries before filing
  • Each person signs their own form, notarized, and you file them all together
  • If even one person refuses to sign, a bond will likely be required
Tip: Call (585) 371-3609 ahead to confirm hours. Ask: "Is there a first-time filer checklist?" Clerks are generally very helpful.
2Court
Receive Letters Testamentary
Your official legal authority as Executor
Weeks 2–4 ›

What This Step Is

"Letters Testamentary" is the official document issued by the court proving you have legal authority to act on behalf of the estate. Without it, banks will not speak with you about accounts.

The Notice of Probate — A Blocker

The court will not deliver your Letters until you complete this step. After filing, you must:

  • Mail a Notice of Probate (Form P-6) to every beneficiary and legatee who hasn't already signed a waiver
  • File an Affidavit of Mailing with the court proving you sent it
  • Each mailing must include a copy of the Will
  • Proofs of service of any citation must be filed at least 2 working days before the return date

What Happens After That

  • Court typically takes 2–4 weeks total to issue Letters after all filings are complete
  • Order 8–10 certified copies at $6 each — every bank and institution wants their own
  • If anyone is under disability (minor, incapacitated), the court will appoint a Guardian Ad Litem before proceeding
This is the "golden ticket." Once you have it, you can legally access, manage, and eventually distribute all estate assets. Keep originals safe.
3Inventory
Inventory All Assets
List accounts, property & notify institutions
Ongoing ›

What This Step Is

Create a complete picture of everything she owned — every account, property, and policy. Her last 2 years of tax returns are the single best "map" of all her financial accounts.

What To Find

  • Bank accounts: Checking, savings, CDs — note Joint or POD designations (those transfer automatically outside probate)
  • Investments: 401(k), IRA, brokerage accounts, physical stock certificates
  • Life insurance: Call each company with policy number to file a claim
  • Real estate: Mortgage statements, property tax bills, HELOC loans
  • Vehicle: Title and registration — contact NYS DMV to transfer
  • Debts: Credit cards, car loans, recent medical bills

The Distributee Family Tree

The petition requires classifying surviving relatives in legal priority order. The court needs to know who falls into each category:

  • 1st: Spouse
  • 2nd: Children (including adopted, non-marital, and their descendants)
  • 3rd: Parents
  • 4th: Siblings and their descendants (nieces/nephews)
  • 5th: Grandparents
  • 6th: Aunts/Uncles and first cousins
  • 7th: First cousins once removed

If only distant relatives survive (grandparents or further), an Affidavit of Heirship is also required.

Notify Locally

  • RG&E (Electric/Gas) — keep ON: (585) 546-1900
  • Monroe County Water — keep ON
  • Social Security Administration — notify of death; return any post-death payments
4Bank
Open an Estate Bank Account
Keep estate money separate from yours
After Letters ›

What This Step Is

Open a bank account in the name of the estate (e.g., "Estate of [Name]"). All estate money flows through here — this protects you legally and makes the final accounting straightforward.

How To Do It

  • Bring your Letters Testamentary and your own ID to any bank
  • Request a basic estate checking account — nothing fancy needed
  • Get an EIN for the estate at IRS.gov — free, takes 5 minutes online
  • Transfer her existing accounts into this new estate account
Never mix funds. Even reimbursing yourself for expenses should come from the estate account with a documented receipt.
5Debts
Settle Debts & Taxes
Pay valid claims from estate funds — not yours
Months 1–6 ›

What This Step Is

Before distributing anything to heirs, valid debts must be paid from the estate account. You are not personally responsible for her debts — creditors can only claim against estate assets, not yours.

What To Pay

  • Funeral and burial costs — paid first, by law
  • Final utility bills and mortgage — keep property current
  • Valid credit card and medical debts — verify each one
  • Federal & NY State final income taxes — file her last Form 1040
  • NY Estate Tax — only if estate exceeds ~$7.16M (2024 threshold)

NY Notice to Creditors

You can publish a "Notice to Creditors" in a local paper (Democrat & Chronicle). This starts a 7-month clock — creditors who don't file within that window lose their claim, protecting you at distribution.

If a debt seems wrong — inflated bills, unrecognized accounts — you have the right to dispute. An estate attorney can help.
6Final
Distribute Assets & Close Estate
Follow the Will, then file final court papers
✓ Finish Line ›

What This Step Is

Once all debts are paid and the creditor notice period has passed, distribute what remains to beneficiaries exactly as the Will directs. Then file a final accounting with the court and formally close the estate.

Distribution Process

  • Pay each heir — get a signed receipt and release from everyone
  • Real estate: file a new deed at Monroe County Clerk's Office (39 W. Main St)
  • Vehicles: transfer title at Monroe County DMV
  • Transfer remaining investment accounts per Will instructions

Closing the Estate

  • File a Final Account with Surrogate's Court — all income, expenses, distributions
  • Court issues a discharge order — your official release from all Executor duties
  • Close the estate bank account
You're done. Keep all records for at least 3–5 years after the court discharge.
Special Situations — Read If They Apply
💍Spouse
Surviving Spouse's Right of Election
Applies if she was married at time of death
If Applicable ›

What This Is

Under New York law (EPTL §5-1.1), a surviving spouse has the right to elect against the Will — meaning they can claim a statutory share of the estate (roughly 1/3) regardless of what the Will says. This must be addressed before distributing anything.

What To Do

  • The surviving spouse must file a Notice of Election within 6 months of Letters being issued (or 2 years from death, whichever is earlier)
  • If the spouse waives this right, they sign a Waiver or Release of the Right of Election
  • This is one area where consulting an estate attorney is strongly recommended
Don't distribute to other heirs until the spouse's election period has passed or they have signed a formal waiver.
🏛️Charity
Charity Named in the Will
Triggers mandatory NY Attorney General notice
If Applicable ›

What This Is

If any charity receives a share of the estate (especially a residuary share), New York law requires you to send a Notice of Probate to the NY State Attorney General. This is a hard legal requirement, not optional.

What To Do

  • Review the Will carefully for any charitable gifts or bequests
  • If found, the court clerk can provide the correct form and mailing address for the AG
  • File proof of mailing with the court
👶Minor
Minor or Disabled Beneficiary
Requires Guardian Ad Litem & extra court steps
If Applicable ›

What This Is

If any beneficiary is a minor (under 18) or legally disabled (incapacitated, in an institution), the court automatically appoints a Guardian Ad Litem to represent their interests before proceeding.

What The Petition Must Include For Each Minor

  • Name, birth date, relationship to decedent, and home address
  • Name of person they live with
  • Whether a court-appointed guardian exists (if so, submit proof of appointment)
  • Whether parents are living
  • A copy of the birth certificate may be required
You cannot distribute a minor's inheritance directly to them. The Guardian Ad Litem or a custodian account must receive it on their behalf.
📋c.t.a.
If the Named Executor Can't Serve
Administrator c.t.a. — a different process
If Applicable ›

What This Is

If the Executor named in the Will has died, resigned, or been removed — and you are stepping in to complete administration — you are not filing a standard Probate Petition. Instead you file for Letters of Administration c.t.a. (with the will annexed) using Form CTA-1 under SCPA §1418 and §1419.

Key Differences

  • Flat filing fee of $45 (not the standard estate-value schedule)
  • You must prove the original executor's office is vacant — by death certificate, proof of resignation, or court removal order
  • Petitioner must be a beneficiary or residuary beneficiary of the Will to be eligible
  • File using Form CTA-1 instead of P-1, and renunciation Form CTA-3 instead of P-11
  • The file number stays the same as the original probate proceeding
Still have the original probate file number? You'll need it — all new filings must reference the same case number as the original proceeding.

Estate Attorney Fees — Monroe County

Hourly billing: Most Rochester estate attorneys charge $200–$500/hr. Simple estates typically run $1,500–$3,000 total.

Flat fee: Some offer $3,500+ for straightforward probate — often the better deal for peace of mind.

Who pays? Attorney fees are paid from the estate account — not your personal money.
